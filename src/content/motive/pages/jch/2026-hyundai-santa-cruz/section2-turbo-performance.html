The 2026 Santa Cruz and 2026 Frontier Turbo Power & Performance

The powertrain philosophies of the Santa Cruz and Frontier are as distinct as their chassis designs, offering buyers a choice between modern versatility and traditional, standardized power.

Hyundai provides two advanced engine options for the Santa Cruz. The standard powertrain, found in the SE, SEL, and SEL Activity trims, is a 2.5-liter four-cylinder engine featuring both Gasoline Direct Injection (GDI) and Multi-Port Injection (MPI). This engine produces a capable 191 horsepower and 181 lb-ft of torque, making it an efficient and affordable choice for those who prioritize fuel economy and utility for lighter tasks. For those seeking a significant performance upgrade, the XRT and Limited trims come standard with a 2.5-liter turbocharged four-cylinder trims come standard with a 2.5-liter turbocharged four-cylinder engine. This powerhouse generates 281 horsepower and a formidable 311 lb-ft of torque. Critically, this peak torque is available at a low 1,700 RPM, translating to immediate, off-the-line responsiveness that makes the Santa Cruz feel exceptionally quick in city traffic and powerful when merging onto I-44. For 2026, both engines are paired with a smooth 8-speed automatic transmission.

Nissan takes a simpler approach, equipping every 2026 Frontier with a single, potent engine: a 3.8-liter direct-injection V6 that produces 310 horsepower and 281 lb-ft of torque, managed by a 9-speed automatic transmission. This standardization is a strong selling point for buyers who believe a truck, by definition, should have V6 power. The engine is smooth and provides ample thrust for any situation. However, its peak torque arrives much later in the rev range, at 4,400 RPM. This means the engine needs to be worked harder to access its full strength, resulting in a more traditional truck-like feel that is less immediately responsive from a standstill compared to the Santa Cruz's turbocharged option.

This creates a fascinating performance dynamic. While the Frontier boasts a higher peak horsepower figure, the turbocharged Santa Cruz delivers more torque and delivers it much earlier. In the typical 1,500 to 3,500 RPM range of everyday driving, the Santa Cruz Turbo is operating at its peak pulling power, while the Frontier is still building momentum. For pulling away from a stoplight, passing on a two-lane highway, or effortlessly cruising, the Santa Cruz's low-end torque provides a tangible advantage in real-world usability.
