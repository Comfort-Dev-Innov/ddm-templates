Myth 1 "Store Visits" Mean Real Traffic ◆

Picture this: You're reviewing your December performance. You log into Google Ads, filter to your local campaigns, and your stomach drops.

Store visits are down 75%. From 144 visits to just 36. Your cost per conversion jumped almost 20%. Total conversions dropped 18%.

Time to panic, right? Time to completely overhaul your strategy? Not so fast.

Here's what actually happened:

We pulled up a dealership account spending about $13,500 on their filtered local campaigns. When we looked at the overall conversion numbers, it showed that we were down 120 conversions compared to the previous period (from 678 to 557). Cost per conversion increased nearly 20%. Any marketing manager would be sweating.

When we segmented by conversion action and looked at the individual conversion types, most were roughly on par with the previous period. Some were even up. This big drop was almost entirely from one conversion type: Store Visits.

Store visits had plummeted from 144 to 36—a 75% decrease.

Now, if that were real, this dealership's showroom would be a ghost town. Their sales would be tanking, and foot traffic would have evaporated.

But it didn't. The business is doing fine, and actual foot traffic is stable.

Why Store Visits data is worthless

This isn't an isolated incident. I see this pattern across accounts constantly. For one month, store visits are really good, but for the next month, they're completely off. The fluctuations never match business reality.

The problem is that Store Visits is a modeled conversion action that Google automatically populates in local business accounts. It uses location data and machine learning to estimate when someone who clicked your ad later visited your physical location.

It sounds like a great metric in theory, but in practice, it's unreliable and muddies up your conversion data.

Now, when we see accounts where Store Visits are creating panic or false confidence, we write them off. They're not good. They're not reliable. And they shouldn't drive your decision-making.

So how do you fix this?

Segment your conversions by conversion action in Google Ads. Look at what's actually happening with your verifiable conversion types, e.g., form fills, trackable calls, chat initiations. If store visits fluctuate wildly but everything else is stable, you know the problem isn't your marketing. It's Google's modeling.

Myth 2 Your GA4 Dashboard Shows All Your Conversions ◆

Google strongly recommends you turn on Data Signals.

Sounds smart, in theory, right? More data is better. Google's recommendation must be the way to go. Except turning on Data Signals might be hiding up to 40% of your actual conversions.

I'm going to show you data from a car dealer running lead generation campaigns. Same time period (last 28 days). However, we set up two separate GA4 properties with different settings.

Property 1 - Data signals turned ON (Google's recommendation):
  • 778 total conversions
  • 6 visible conversion events
  • 412 total users
  • Orange warning icon: "Thresholding applied"
Property 2 - Data signals turned OFF:
  • 1,095 total conversions
  • 12 visible conversion events
  • 579 total users
  • Green checkmark: "Unsampled report - based on 100% of available data"
That's 317 more conversions (a 40% difference!) just from changing one setting. Same website, same traffic, and the same time period.

To make matters worse, the first property showed only 6 conversion events, while the second showed 12. Half of the conversion types weren't even visible when Data Signals was on.

Understanding the Problem

So what's going on here?

When you turn on Data Signals (which Google recommends during setup), you're allowing Google to blend your actual website data with modeled data from their broader user signals.

This triggers something called "thresholding." You'll see a little orange caution cone at the top of your reports with a message: "Google Analytics has applied thresholding to one or more cards in this report."

According to Google, thresholding is applied "to prevent anyone viewing a report from inferring the identity or sensitive information of individual users." When you have lower user counts in your date range and Data Signals is enabled, Google starts hiding data.

The data isn't gone; it happened. Real people converted on your website. However, Google won't show it to you.

So what do you do to fix it?

Here's what I recommend:

Create two GA4 properties under the same account:

  1. Property with Data Signals ON - Use this for building remarketing audiences (Google requires Data Signals for audience creation in Google Ads)
  2. Property with Data Signals OFF - Use this for actual reporting and analysis
🏷️

Step 1: Label Your Properties

Label them clearly: "Business Name - Data Signals" and "Business Name - Clean Data" so you always know which property you're looking at.

⚙️

Step 2: Configure Data Signals

To change this setting, click the admin gear icon, go to Setup Assistant, then "Customize data collection and display." You'll see the Data Signals toggle.

📊

Step 3: Use the Right Property

When you want accurate conversion data, look at the clean property. When you need to build audiences for remarketing, use the Data Signals property. It's extra setup work, but it's the only way to see what's actually happening on your website.

Myth 3 "Phone Calls" Are Actually Phone Calls ◆

When you see "Phone Calls" in your Google Ads dashboard, you probably think it means "people who called my business and spoke to someone."

That's not what it means.

According to Google's own definition: "Phone calls are the number of times people clicked on a phone number displayed in an ad on a mobile device or manually dialed the Google forwarding number."

Read that carefully. It's the number of times someone clicked or dialed. Not the number of conversations. Not even the number of times your phone rang.

Just clicks and dials.

Here's what we discovered:

For this business, we installed CallRail, a third-party call tracking system. We set up a local tracking number and made sure it was properly connected to all the ad assets. Then we listened to every single call that came through.

Now, you might be thinking: "But I don't use the Phone Calls metric. I use 'Call from Ads' conversions, which require the call to last longer than 30 seconds."

That's better. But it's still not telling you what you think it's telling you.

Why This Happens (And Why It Matters)

We don't entirely understand why this discrepancy occurs at this scale, and it may not affect every account. Your phone call metrics might be more accurate.

But here's what we know for certain: this happens. We see it, we've documented it. And if you're not verifying your phone call data with an independent tracking system, you have no way of knowing if it's happening to you.

The client kept telling us: "We're not actually getting the phone calls you guys are saying we're getting." Turns out they were right.

How to Fix Misleading Google Ads Data

  • Segment by conversion action in Google Ads to see which conversions are actually changing
  • Ignore Store Visits data in your decision-making
  • Focus on verifiable conversion actions: forms, trackable calls, chat conversations
  • If Store Visits fluctuate wildly but other metrics are stable, trust the other metrics
  • Create two GA4 properties under your account
  • Turn Data Signals OFF on one property for clean reporting
  • Keep Data Signals ON on the other for remarketing audiences
  • Watch for the orange "thresholding applied" warning cone
  • Compare both properties regularly to understand what data you're missing
  • Implement third-party call tracking (We recommend CallRail)
  • Listen to actual calls to verify quality and count
  • Understand Google's definitions: "Phone Calls" = clicks/dials, "Call from Ads" = 30+ seconds
  • Report internally on actual conversations, not platform metrics
  • Cross-reference Google's numbers with your call tracking system
Another important thing to do is to always question everything and double-check everything.

Not everything that Google recommends as a default setting is good. You need to really explore outside the bounds to get accurate reporting.

Test these things for yourself.

If you have events and conversions set up properly in your account, try the dual GA4 property approach. Compare Store Visits to your actual foot traffic. Install call tracking and see how it aligns with Google's numbers.

We have plenty of resources on YouTube for setting up events in Google Tag Manager and pushing them to GA4 if you need help with these setups.
