How Does Google Ads Actually Work for Dealerships?

At its core, Google Ads connects what buyers are searching for with the inventory your dealership wants to promote. For example, when someone types "Mazda dealer near me", Google instantly runs an auction to decide which ads to display and decides which ads to show based on how closely every campaign created matches that search. These could be based on keywords, budget, ad relevance, and landing page experience.

Google Ads auction showing how dealership campaigns compete

For dealerships, this happens in three main steps:

  1. Inventory + Setup
    Your vehicles and offers feed into Google Merchant Center. From there, Google automatically generates ads that show make, model, price, and mileage directly in search results. This makes your inventory discoverable by high-intent shoppers the moment they search.
  2. Auction + Placement
    Every search triggers a lightning-fast auction. Google weighs factors like your bid, ad quality, and relevance to decide if â€” and where â€” your ad appears. A well-structured campaign with relevant keywords and strong copy can outperform higher-spending competitors.
  3. Click + Conversion
    Once a potential buyer clicks, they're taken to your website or a specific vehicle detail page. From there, the goal is to turn that visit into a test drive, call, or lead form. Tracking those actions with conversion tags and GA4 is what separates "clicks" from real sales opportunities.

5 Types of Google Ads Campaigns for Dealerships

1. Classic Search Campaigns

You'll typically find most dealerships launching this campaign type as part of their advertising, which is why we call them the "classic" search ads.

They're text-based ads that appear when buyers look for specific vehicles, dealerships, or offers.

Example of a classic search ad for car dealerships

Search ads are ideal for reaching people already in the market for a car or service (high-intent buyers) and are best for generating leads from active shoppers ready to buy or schedule a test drive. You also have more control over the keywords you target, meaning you can focus spend on the terms that matter most to your dealership.

Just ensure you always use clear calls to action and align your ad copy with your landing pages (e.g., "Book a Test Drive," "Check Availability.")

2. Vehicle Ads (VFAs)

If there's one ad type made for dealerships, it's this one.

Vehicle Ads pull real-time inventory from your Google Merchant Center feed (make, model, price, mileage, and images) and show it directly in Google Search results. So when someone searches "new Honda CRV," for example, they see what's actually sitting on the lot before they even click on the dealership website.

They're designed for people who are ready to buy, not just browse.

Vehicle Ads now run inside Performance Max. That means your inventory connects automatically, and Google's AI decides when and where your listings should appear. Whether that's in Search, Maps, or Discover. It's all powered by your feed.

But automation doesn't mean "hands off."

Sometimes, these ads can appear outside Shopping placements, even when set to run there. You might notice odd results like phone call conversions, which Vehicle Ads don't technically support. When that happens, check your Listing Groups and Insights tab to see where those clicks are really coming from.

Vehicle Ads display your live inventory right in Google Search results when shoppers look for nearby vehicles

To keep your Vehicle Ads running smoothly:

  • Make sure your Merchant Center feed is verified and complete. Google requires details like make, model, year, VIN, price, and mileage.
  • Update it every day. Missing images or old prices can stop your listings from showing.
  • Add extras like financing options or special offers. Complete feeds tend to perform better.
  • Keep an eye on data quality. Dealers who update daily see stronger results than those who don't.

You can also preview your ads.

To do so, open your Performance Max campaign, go to an Asset Group, click the three dots (â€¢â€¢â€¢), and choose Share Preview. The link (it's active for two weeks) shows how your inventory appears across Google â€” Search, Maps, Display, and even YouTube.

Vehicle Ads make it easy for buyers to find what you have in stock. Keep your feed clean, check your placements, and let Google do the heavy lifting from there.

3. Performance Max Campaigns (PMax)

Performance Max is Google's most advanced campaign type and helps dealerships reach customers everywhere they browse or search.

So how does it work?

Instead of running separate campaigns for Search, Display, YouTube, Maps, Gmail, and Discover, PMax combines them into one campaign. It uses Google's AI to decide where each ad should appear and which shoppers are most likely to convert â€” all while optimizing toward your set goals.

It gets even smarter when connected to your vehicle feed. That's when Google can automatically match your live inventory to shopper intent, showing the right car to the right buyer at the right time.

But it's important to know that not all PMax campaigns are the same.

  • A Vehicle Ads (VFA) campaign is a simplified version built only around your inventory feed.
  • A full-build PMax campaign includes creative assets (images, videos, headlines, and descriptions) which allow your ads to appear across all six Google networks: Search, YouTube, Gmail, Maps, Display, and Discover.

Because of that reach, many dealership teams find PMax difficult to visualize. One campaign can generate dozens of ad variations across multiple channels, and each format looks slightly different depending on where it appears.

To make PMax work well, focus on what the AI needs most: fresh data and clear signals. Keep your creative assets current, upload short videos and new vehicle images, and refresh ad copy often. Ensure you also use brand exclusions and negative keywords to avoid irrelevant clicks, then let the algorithm learn and optimize.

4. Display Campaigns

Display campaigns help your dealership stay visible even when shoppers aren't actively searching.

These ads appear across Google's network of websites, apps, and YouTube, reaching people while they read the news, watch videos, or browse other sites.

They're perfect for building awareness and retargeting visitors who've already been to your website but haven't converted yet. Think of them as gentle reminders that keep your dealership top of mind.

To get the most out of Display campaigns, use strong visuals: high-quality vehicle images, service photos, or limited-time offers. You can also pair them with simple, direct messages like "View Current Specials" or "Get Pre-Approved Today."

And as always, let performance data guide your next move. Test different visuals, headlines, and placements to see what actually drives clicks and form fills.

5. Video Campaigns

Video campaigns are one of the best ways for dealerships to build brand recognition and connect with buyers on a personal level.

These ads typically run on YouTube and are perfect for vehicle walkarounds, service promos, or seasonal offers. They're great for storytelling and showing the personality behind your dealership and the value of your vehicles.

They work best when tied to model launches, limited-time specials, or community events, helping you stay top-of-mind even before buyers start searching.

We recommend keeping videos short (6â€“15 seconds), mobile-friendly, and ending with a clear CTA like "Visit Us Today" or "Explore Inventory."

How much should a car dealership spend on Google Ads?

The short answer is: enough to hit your sales and service goals without paying for noise.

Most dealers fall somewhere between $1,500â€“$8,000/month for smaller stores and $20,000â€“$50,000+/month for large groups. However, the right number depends on your market, inventory, and priorities (new vs. used, sales vs. service).

Let's make it practical.

1. Start with your goal (not a guess)

  • "We need X more vehicle leads per month."
  • "We want Y more service bookings."
  • "We need to move Z-aged units (e.g., TRD Pro trims) in the first quarter of 2026."

Work backwards from the goal: typical paid search cost per lead in automotive varies by market, but you can model with your own data (or a conservative estimate) to size the daily budget.

Quick model:
Needed incremental leads Ã— Target cost per lead = Monthly Google Ads budget for that objective.

A simple split that works (then you tailor it)

  • 60â€“70% to Sales (Search + Performance Max with Vehicle Ads)
  • 15â€“20% to Service (Local/Display, some Search)
  • 10â€“15% to Brand/Retargeting (Video + Display)

If you only sell, push more into Search + PMax. If service is a profit center, ring-fence budget for it so Sales doesn't eat it.

National "averages" break once you step into competitive metros. If your market is Chicago-level competitive, expect to spend above the national mean.

2. Don't bundle everything. Budget by what matters

One catch-all campaign lets the algorithm chase the heaviest demand (Camry/Corolla) and starve lower-demand, high-priority units (e.g., TRD Pro). Fix it by splitting campaigns so you can put money exactly where you need movement.

  • Create separate Vehicle Ads campaigns by priority: Aged or high-margin models, certified vs. non-certified, used Toyota vs. used Honda, etc.
  • Give each its own daily budget and its own radius (12â€“15 miles for city stores, wider for suburbs or new-car pushes).
  • Search demand isn't equal. Breaking campaigns lets you protect budget for vehicles that won't win the auction on their own.

3. Keep an eye on where money actually goes

Even when you intend "Shopping-only," PMax can extend into Search/Display and report odd conversions (like phone calls) that VAs don't natively support. So budget isn't just "how much," It's where it's landing.

To stay on top of this,

  • Check Listing Groups vs. total clicks to see how many actually came from product listings.
  • Review the Insights tab weekly to spot branded or off-target categories.
  • Use brand exclusions and negative keywords to curb waste.

4. Make location part of the budget conversation

Most sales come from within ~25 miles. Use Presence (in or regularly in your location) rather than "presence or interest" when choosing your location, and widen the radius only for the models where buyers travel farther (new, hard-to-find trims).

How to Set Up a Google Ads Account for Your Dealership

Before running your first campaign, make sure your Google Ads account is properly configured. A clean setup makes tracking easier, improves data accuracy, and helps every dollar work harder.

1) Create or access your Google Ads account

Visit ads.google.com and sign in.

If your store already uses Google Business Profile, Google Analytics, or Merchant Center, sign in with the same account. It keeps everything connected under one profile.

Once inside, click "New Google Ads Account."

At the setup prompt, choose "Switch to Expert Mode" at the bottom of the page. This gives full control instead of Google's default "Smart" setup, which limits access to key settings.

2) Link your core platforms

Go to Tools â†’ Linked Accounts and connect these right away:

Platform Why It Matters
Google Analytics 4 (GA4) Tracks calls, chats, and form submissions from your website.
Google Merchant Center Syncs your live inventory (make, model, price, VIN, mileage, photos) for Vehicle Ads.
Google Business Profile Enables location extensions and, when eligible, store-visit tracking.

If you operate multiple rooftops, give each its own Merchant Center sub-account so budgets, feeds, and reporting stay separate.

3) Verify billing and access

Under Settings â†’ Billing, enter your payment method, time zone, and currency.

Then open Tools â†’ Access and Security to invite your marketing team or agency. Use company emails only so account ownership always stays with the dealership.

4) Set up conversion tracking

Go to Tools â†’ Conversions and define what success looks like for your store. Some typical dealership conversions are:

  • Click-to-call from ad extensions or mobile site
  • Lead form submissions
  • Chat messages sent
  • Store visits (when eligible through your Business Profile)

Avoid counting page views or auto-opened chat boxes as conversions. They inflate numbers without reflecting intent.

For website actions, Google will generate a code snippet. Ask your web provider to install it sitewide or through Google Tag Manager.

5) Create your first campaign

From the main dashboard, click the blue "+ New Campaign" button.

When asked for an objective, you can start with Leads or select "Create a campaign without goals guidance."

Then:

  • Choose Search as the campaign type.
  • Select Maximize Clicks if you have no conversion history yet.
  • Set a bid cap that fits your market.
  • Turn off Search Partners and Display Network to avoid wasted reach.
  • Set location to Presence only within your dealership radius.
  • Add focused keywords and ad groups for each model, service, or offer.

6) Connect your inventory feed (for Vehicle Ads)

If you plan to run Vehicle Ads or Performance Max campaigns, link your Google Merchant Center feed.

Make sure every listing includes: Make, Model, Year, VIN, Price, Mileage, Image, and Landing Page URL, and refresh your feed daily to keep ads live and compliant.

7) Organize your account before launch

Keep your structure simple and scalable:

Level Example Purpose
Account The single store or rooftop
Campaigns Sales, Used, Service, Brand
Ad Groups Model or offer focus (e.g., Rogue, Pathfinder, Oil Change)
Ads + Assets Final messages and CTAs shoppers see

Consistent naming conventions make future optimization and reporting much easier.

8) Launch and verify tracking

Start small. Set a token daily budget while confirming impressions, clicks, and conversions are recording correctly. Once verified, scale to your real budget and let the campaign run for a week before making adjustments.

Google Ads Setup Checklist: Do's and Don'ts for Dealerships

A well-built campaign structure is only half the job. The other half is knowing what not to check, skip, or assume.

Here's a quick list of what to do (and what to avoid) when setting up your first Google Ads campaign for your dealership.

Do

  • Choose "Leads" or "Without Goals Guidance," but review each setting manually.
  • Track real intent actions: chat submits (after phone entry), click-to-call, and lead form completions.
  • Start with Search campaigns before expanding into Performance Max.
  • Use Maximize Clicks if you don't have conversion history yet, then shift to conversion-based bidding once data builds.
  • Keep networks separate. Run Search, Display, and Video individually.
  • Target by Presence only to reach real local shoppers.
  • Use Exact and Phrase match types before testing Broad.
  • Group keywords by intent: New, Used, Certified, or Service.
  • Fill all ad assets (15 headlines, 4 descriptions, sitelinks, callouts, business name, and logo).
  • Start with a low daily budget while confirming tracking accuracy.

Don't

  • Launch with Performance Max.
  • Count page views or auto-opened chats as conversions.
  • Enable "Bid for new customers only" in a new account.
  • Check Search Partners or Display Network inside a Search campaign.
  • Turn on Display Expansion for Search.
  • Select "Presence or interest" in your location settings.
  • Mix unrelated goals (Service, Used, New) into one campaign.
  • Accept Google's auto-generated keywords or ads without reviewing them.
