The Five Essential Google Ad Campaign Types

At DDM, here's the framework we use:

  1. Vehicle Listing Ads (VLAs)
  2. New Model Classic Search
  3. Branding
  4. Competitor
  5. Service

These aren't Google's technical campaign types (Search, Display, Performance Max, etc.). They're automotive-specific strategies that map to how car buyers actually search and how dealerships make money. Google gives you the tools, but these five campaign types are the blueprint for using them effectively in the car business.

Let's look at each one in detail.

1. Vehicle Listing Ads

VLAs should be your number one priority. This is where you start, and it's where most of your early budget should go.

So what do they look like?

If you search for a car model on Google, you will notice some ads at the very top with vehicle images. Those are VLAs.

Example of Vehicle Listing Ads appearing at the top of Google search results

Each listing shows:

  • A photo of the actual vehicle
  • Year, make, model, trim
  • Sale price and MSRP
  • Whether it's new or pre-owned
  • The dealership name
  • City location

It's essentially Google Shopping ads for cars. Google took the same format they use for consumer products and applied it to automotive, and it's been a game-changer for dealers willing to use it correctly.

VLAs get you top placement at a fraction of the cost. CPCs typically range from $0.50 to $2.00 in major metros like Chicago. Compare that to traditional search ads, where you're paying $3 to $6 per click, and the math becomes obvious. With it, you get prime real estate (the first thing someone sees when they search) without paying premium prices.

How to create effective Vehicle Listing Ads (VLAs)

Most people make the same mistake when setting up Vehicle Listing Ads. They create one big campaign and dump all their inventory into itâ€”F-150s, Escapes, Bronco Sports, everything together.

That might feel efficient, but it actually hurts performance.

Google's system works better when you give it focus. So instead of one catch-all campaign, run separate ones for each model: one for F-150s, another for Escapes, another for Bronco Sports. That way, Google can learn faster, optimize smarter, and you can put more budget behind the models that are actually moving.

Key Takeaway: The best part is you don't need to split campaigns by new and used. Google already handles that automatically. If someone searches "new Ford F-150 for sale," your new inventory shows. If they search "used Ford F-150," they'll see your used listings with mileage and pricing.

Vehicle Feeds on Search Ads vs. Vehicle Listing Ads

VFSAs (Vehicle Feeds on Search Ads) are the newest development in automotive advertising, and most dealers don't even know they exist yet.

This ad type combines three components into one ad unit:

  • The top section is your traditional search ad: headlines and descriptions.
  • The bottom section contains your assets, such as site links, call buttons, and all the extensions you're used to.
  • The middle sectionâ€”the most important partâ€”is your vehicle ads. Your actual inventory, displayed right in the middle of a search ad.
With it, you're getting a search ad (customizable copy, extensions, control) combined with the visual impact of vehicle listings. It's the best of both formats in a single placement. While VLAs trigger on model searches (e.g., "new Honda CR-V" or "used Ford F-150 for sale"), VFSAs trigger on dealer and brand searches (e.g., "Honda dealers near me" or "Audi SUV dealers near me").

VFSAs combine traditional search ads with visual vehicle inventory

How to Get Vehicle Feeds on Search Ads (VFSAs)

Here's the thing: you can't just turn VFSAs on.

We spent a lot of money testing different configurations before we realized it wasn't something we could enable ourselves.

  1. You need a Merchant Center account with an active vehicle feed (you already have this if you're running VLAs)
  2. You need advertiser-provided or automatic assets enabled
  3. You need to request beta program access from the specialized Google support team

2. Classic Search Ads

Classic search ads are the traditional blue hyperlink with a "Sponsored" tag above it.

Classic search ads feature headlines, descriptions, and site links

It has:

  • A headline
  • Description text
  • Site links
  • Your dealership name

When to Use Classic Search Ads

Search Ads campaigns should run alongside your VLAs. They complement each other. VLAs get you the visual placement at the top, but classic search ads give you more space to communicate your value proposition. You can highlight:

  • Special financing offers
  • Trade-in bonuses
  • Years in business
  • Inventory size
  • Why someone should choose you over the dealer down the street

Just make sure you don't repeat the make and model over and over; use that space to actually sell the click. Give people a reason to buy from you. This could be a deal or an incentive.

3. Branding Campaigns

When we talk about branding in Google Ads, we're really talking about three kinds of searches.

1. Generic dealer keywords

These are searches like "Ford dealer near me" or "Honda dealer Chicago." These buyers aren't looking for a specific store yet; they just want a nearby dealership that sells the brand they're interested in.

2. Generic brand terms

These are broader searches like "Ford" or "Honda CR-V." These usually come from people still in the research phase, comparing models or learning more before they visit a dealer.

3. Your specific dealership name

These are searches such as "Galpin Ford" or "Curry Ford." These shoppers already know who they want.

Each of these signals a different level of intent, which is why they deserve their own campaigns. The message and targeting that work for someone researching "Ford CR-V" won't connect with someone searching "Curry Ford near me."

Branding campaigns capture local intent and reinforce trust

The image above is an example of a branding campaign doing exactly what it should: capturing local intent, showing inventory, and reinforcing trust with community credibility.

If you skip this one, other dealerships may start bidding on your name and brand terms, showing up for searches that should have led buyers directly to your store. It's one of the easiest ways to lose traffic and one of the simplest to fix.

4. Competitor Campaigns

Competitor campaigns mean bidding on another dealership's name. Ford Dealer A bids on "Ford Dealer B" so their ad shows up when someone searches for their competitor. Competitor campaigns are also expensive. CPCs can be high, and the ROI isn't always there.

When it works

  • Your competitor is weak on defending their own name
  • You've already maxed out your other campaign types
  • You have budget to spare
  • Your market is highly competitive, and you need every edge

If you're on a tight budget, skip this. If you're trying to decide between optimizing your VLAs or launching competitor campaigns, optimize your VLAs every time.

How to identify Competitor Ads

If you see an ad with a "Sponsored" tag when you search for a specific dealership name, it's a competitor ad. The organic results (the ones without "Sponsored") are free.

5. Service Campaigns

If your service department can handle more volume, service campaigns are worth the investment.

Service campaigns go after searches like:

  • "Jeep oil change"
  • "Honda service near me"
  • "Ford parts"
  • "Engine rebuild"

You can get as specific as you want based on search volume and budget. Oil changes have high volume and low cost per service. Engine rebuilds have low volume but high value per customer.

Service campaigns focus on specific maintenance and repair searches

Don't try to sell cars in a service ad. Focus on the specific service someone is searching for, make booking easy, and optimize for appointment conversions rather than vehicle sales.

Service campaigns make sense when:

  • Your service department has capacity
  • You have budget beyond vehicle sales campaigns
  • You're competing in a market where service business is up for grabs

If you're already maxing out your service bays, consider holding off. But if you've got openings and techs ready to work, this can drive consistent revenue month after month.

Getting Started With Google Ads

Google Ads for car dealerships isn't rocket science, but it's not simple either. You need to understand which campaigns to run, how to structure them, and where the common pitfalls are.

Start with VLAs. Get those optimized. Once you're getting solid performance there, layer in classic search for new models. Protect your brand with branding campaigns. If you've got budget and your foundation is strong, explore VFSAs. Avoid the Performance Max trap. Use brand exclusions. Don't let negative keywords block your ads. Check your daily budget and location targeting.

Most importantly, don't expect overnight miracles. Build the foundation, optimize consistently, and the results will come.
