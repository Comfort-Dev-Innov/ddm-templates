Myth 1 "Store Visits" Mean Real Traffic ◆

Picture this: You're reviewing your December performance. You log into Google Ads, filter to your local campaigns, and your stomach drops.

Store visits are down 75%. From 144 visits to just 36. Your cost per conversion jumped almost 20%. Total conversions dropped 18%.

Time to panic, right? Time to completely overhaul your strategy? Not so fast.

Here's what actually happened:

We pulled up a dealership account spending about $13,500 on their filtered local campaigns. When we looked at the overall conversion numbers, it showed that we were down 120 conversions compared to the previous period (from 678 to 557). Cost per conversion increased nearly 20%. Any marketing manager would be sweating.

When we segmented by conversion action and looked at the individual conversion types, most were roughly on par with the previous period. Some were even up. This big drop was almost entirely from one conversion type: Store Visits.

Store visits had plummeted from 144 to 36—a 75% decrease.

Now, if that were real, this dealership's showroom would be a ghost town. Their sales would be tanking, and foot traffic would have evaporated.

But it didn't. The business is doing fine, and actual foot traffic is stable.

Why Store Visits data is worthless

This isn't an isolated incident. I see this pattern across accounts constantly. For one month, store visits are really good, but for the next month, they're completely off. The fluctuations never match business reality.

The problem is that Store Visits is a modeled conversion action that Google automatically populates in local business accounts. It uses location data and machine learning to estimate when someone who clicked your ad later visited your physical location.

It sounds like a great metric in theory, but in practice, it's unreliable and muddies up your conversion data.

Now, when we see accounts where Store Visits are creating panic or false confidence, we write them off. They're not good. They're not reliable. And they shouldn't drive your decision-making.

So how do you fix this?

Segment your conversions by conversion action in Google Ads. Look at what's actually happening with your verifiable conversion types, e.g., form fills, trackable calls, chat initiations. If store visits fluctuate wildly but everything else is stable, you know the problem isn't your marketing. It's Google's modeling.
